01

Foundations

AI isn't one thing. It's a stack.

Different layers handle different decisions. When a conversation about AI feels muddled, it's usually because two people are talking about different layers without realizing it.

  1. L1

    Model providers

    The companies that train and host the underlying models — OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta, and others. Think of them as cloud infrastructure for intelligence: you rent access, you don't own the engine.

    ↗
  2. L2

    Models / LLMs

    The raw engine. Text in, text (or code, or tool calls) out. Capability, cost, and speed vary widely — and keep improving.

    ↗
  3. L3

    Apps & interfaces

    Where humans actually interact: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, custom internal tools. This is the chrome around the engine — most of the UX you experience lives here.

    ↗
  4. L4

    APIs

    The machine interface. How our own software calls a model programmatically — the layer that makes AI embeddable rather than just visitable.

    ↗
  5. L5

    Workflows

    Sequences where AI is one step among many. Usually a mix of deterministic logic and AI-powered reasoning. This is where most durable business value lives today.

    ↗
  6. L6

    Agents

    Systems that use AI to plan, act, and iterate across tools toward a goal — with varying degrees of autonomy. Newer, higher ceiling, more care required.

    ↗
  7. L7

    Humans in the loop

    Where we review, approve, correct, or override. Not a limitation to engineer away — often the most important design choice in the whole stack.

    ↗

02

The key distinction

Generative AI produces. Agentic AI pursues.

Of every distinction on this page, this is the one most worth committing to memory. The difference is not "smart vs. smarter" — it's content generation vs. goal-directed action.

Generative

Helps you create.

Systems that turn a prompt into content — text, code, images, summaries, explanations.

Mode
Reactive. You ask; it responds.
Good at
Drafts, summaries, explanations, translation, first-pass analysis.
Shape of work
Bounded, single-turn or short-turn.
Examples
A marketing brief. A one-page explainer. A rewritten email.

Agentic

Helps you execute.

Systems that take a goal, plan steps, use tools, check their own work, and iterate.

Mode
Proactive. You set a goal; it works through steps.
Good at
Multi-step research, tool-heavy tasks, coordinated operations.
Shape of work
Longer horizon, multiple tools, self-correction loops.
Examples
"Research this lead and draft outreach." "Find mismatches across these reports and flag them."

Many modern systems combine both modes. A useful agent is often just a generative model plus tools, memory, and a loop.

03

Anatomy

What's under the hood of an AI agent.

An agent is not a chatbot with a fancy label. It's a system with parts that happen to use a language model as its core reasoner — and the model is only one of them.

Ingredients of a useful agent

Agents fail predictably when any of these are thin. Most of the work of building one is the work of getting these right.

  • Clear instructions. What it's for, what it isn't.
  • Relevant knowledge. The documents, policies, and data it can reference.
  • Policies and constraints. What's allowed, what requires human approval.
  • The right tools. APIs, search, file access — whatever the job actually needs.
  • Memory and state. So it can carry information across steps and sessions.
  • Feedback loops. Human review, automated checks, or both.

04

When to use what

Not every process should be agentic.

Some of the highest-value improvements at Trend won't look like AI at all. The design question isn't "can AI do this?" — it's "what level of autonomy fits this task?"

Predictable Ambiguous

Rules-based automation

When steps are predictable and repeatable.

Examples
Syncing data, triggering reminders, conditional routing.
Strengths
Cheap, reliable, auditable.
Limits
Brittle when inputs get messy.

AI-assisted workflow

When steps are mostly known but inputs are messy.

Examples
Classifying inbound requests, extracting info from unstructured text, summarizing a call.
Strengths
Handles ambiguity while staying bounded.
Limits
Still a human-shaped process; AI doesn't run it end-to-end.

Agentic system

When the path isn't fixed and reasoning across tools is required.

Examples
Multi-step research, coordinated prep, composite decisions.
Strengths
Flexibility across unknown-shaped work.
Limits
Harder to debug, demands careful guardrails.

05

The tool landscape

What's actually out there, and what it's for.

The category of tool matters more than the brand name. Each sits at a different layer of the stack and solves a different kind of problem — from the chat window people use every day to the platforms where workflows get built.

Tool What it is What it's good for Why it matters
ChatGPT · Claude Polished chat interfaces to a model. Interactive thinking, drafting, research, exploration. Entry point most people encounter first. Good for ad-hoc work, not for repeatable systems.
Cursor An AI-native IDE. Building software with an always-on collaborator in the editor. Large productivity shift for development work — especially prototyping and internal tools.
Microsoft Copilot · Notion AI AI embedded inside tools we already use. Drafting, summarizing, and analyzing inside Word, Excel, Gmail, Docs, Notion. Meets people where they already work — no new tool to adopt, low friction to roll out.
Perplexity AI-powered search and research. Getting cited, up-to-date answers rather than generic summaries. Starting to replace traditional search for real research tasks where sources matter.
Zapier AI · Make · n8n Automation platforms with AI steps built in. Connecting apps and inserting reasoning between them — without custom code. Where most AI-assisted workflows get built at small-to-mid scale.

06

Model selection

Bigger isn't always better.

Picking a model is an operational decision, not a prestige choice. The right one fits the task, the budget, and the quality bar — not the hype cycle.

Smaller · specialized

Narrow, high-volume, latency- or privacy-sensitive work.

Best fit
Classification, routing, structured extraction, embeddings.
Strengths
Fast, cheap, easier to govern or self-host.
Tradeoffs
Narrower reasoning; less flexible on messy inputs.

General-purpose LLM

Messy reasoning and language-heavy day-to-day work.

Best fit
Drafting, summarization, research, analysis, most workflows.
Strengths
Broad capability, reasonable cost, mature tooling.
Tradeoffs
Overkill for simple structured tasks; under-powered for the hardest ones.

Frontier

The hardest reasoning and highest-stakes outputs.

Best fit
Complex multi-step reasoning, code-heavy agents, ambiguous judgment calls.
Strengths
Top-of-curve capability; keeps improving.
Tradeoffs
Higher cost and latency; reserve for tasks where the capability matters.

The operational question is fitness, not prestige. Match capability to the task — and expect the answer to change as models improve.

07

Useful now

Already doing real work.

AI is already highly useful across a set of real, repeatable business categories. These are the places where value is sitting in plain view — where adoption is mostly a discipline problem, not a capability problem.

  • Language

    Summarization

    Meetings, threads, reports, long documents — distilled into something a person can act on.

  • Language

    Drafting

    First-pass copy, emails, briefs, proposals. Not finished work — fast momentum.

  • Software

    Coding assistance

    Writing, refactoring, and debugging code inside IDEs like Cursor.

  • Structure

    Classification & routing

    Tagging inbound requests, prioritizing tickets, routing work to the right place.

  • Research

    Research support

    Background scans, synthesis across sources, quick briefs on unfamiliar topics.

  • Analysis

    Structured analysis

    Extracting fields, comparing documents, flagging anomalies across data.

  • Integration

    Pulling info across systems

    Connecting scattered context — docs, threads, records — into one coherent view.

  • Execution

    Bounded multi-step work

    Taking a defined process with some ambiguity and running it end-to-end, with review.

What's underneath All of these lean on a single underlying capability: AI now understands natural language dramatically better than older software could. That alone unlocks most of the list.

08

Needs oversight

Impressively capable. Still unreliable.

AI can do remarkable things and still fail in consistent, knowable ways. The right response isn't fear or hype — it's disciplined design.

  1. 01

    Hallucinations

    Models can produce confident, fluent answers that are simply wrong. The fluency is the hazard — errors don't announce themselves.

  2. 02

    Overconfidence

    Tone stays assured even when the model is guessing. Calibration is not a built-in feature — it has to be designed in.

  3. 03

    Brittle autonomy

    The further an agent runs on its own, the more small errors compound. Long unsupervised chains are the highest-risk shape of work.

  4. 04

    Incomplete business context

    Models don't know our priorities, policies, or recent decisions unless we deliberately give them that context. Generic answers follow from generic context.

  5. 05

    Inconsistent judgment

    Similar inputs can produce different outputs. Good enough on average is not the same as reliable on a specific case.

  6. 06

    Ongoing need for evaluation

    Inputs drift, models update, behaviors shift. Quality has to be measured continuously — not once, at launch.

None of this is a reason to hold back. It's a reason to design with grounding, guardrails, and human-in-the-loop review built in from the start.

09

Leadership frame

Humans own the what. AI helps with the how.

This is not a replacement story. It's a leverage story. The best systems pair human intent with machine speed — and leadership still owns purpose and accountability.

Humans still own

  • Goals, priorities, and boundaries
  • Tradeoffs and judgment calls
  • Business context and relationships
  • Ethical and policy constraints
  • Accountability for outcomes

AI is increasingly useful for

  • Generating options and first drafts
  • Executing bounded, well-defined tasks
  • Assisting with analysis and research
  • Running workflows at scale
  • Turning language into structure
Trend should think of AI as an operating lever — not as an autonomous replacement for leadership or judgment.

10

Near-term horizon

What actually changes in the next 12–24 months.

Keeping this short on purpose. The goal is a grounded view, not prediction theater. The next phase isn't better chat — it's more real operational systems.

Now

  • AI mostly used inside chat tools
  • Workflow integration still uneven
  • Agents impressive in demos, cautious in production
  • Evaluation & governance practices still maturing

Next

  • More AI embedded directly into workflows
  • More tool-using agents in real operations
  • More orchestration across specialized components
  • Better human-in-the-loop design patterns
  • More governance, evaluation, and traceability
  • More fit-for-purpose deployment — including smaller reasoning models where they fit

Deliberately out of scope Robotics, quantum, and AGI speculation. Interesting, but not what will change how Trend operates this year.

11

Applying it

How this applies to my role as AI Systems Builder.

Trend created this role because AI is becoming relevant at the workflow and systems level, not just the experimentation level. The opportunity is to identify where AI can improve real work, choose the right level of automation, design guardrails, and bring the right tools into the stack.

Focus area

Operational workflows

Reduce manual overhead on the 20% of work that eats 80% of the time.

Focus area

Information handling

Turn scattered context — docs, threads, reports — into something consistently accessible.

Focus area

Smarter automations

Move selected processes from rule-based to AI-assisted where ambiguity blocks scale.

Focus area

Development tooling

Use AI-native tooling to ship internal software faster and with fewer regressions.

Working principle Applied operational value, not innovation theater. Some of the best work is knowing where not to apply AI.
